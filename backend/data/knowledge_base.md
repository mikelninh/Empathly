## CHUNK: love | liebe | tinh yeu | tamil | arabic | greek

### The Many Words for Love

Most languages have a single word where others have many nuanced concepts.
German uses "Liebe" for almost everything romantic, while Vietnamese, Tamil, Arabic, and Greek each draw
precise distinctions that reveal what a culture values in relationships.

**German**
- **Liebe** — the general word for love (romantic, familial, spiritual)
- **Zuneigung** — affection, a warm feeling without full intensity of love
- **Verliebtheit** — the state of being "in love", the initial falling phase
- **Innigkeit** — deep emotional closeness and tenderness, often for long-term bonds
- **Geborgenheit** — feeling of safe, warm belonging (no English equivalent) — a foundational emotional need

**Vietnamese (5 core love words)**
- **yêu** — romantic love, passionate love between partners. Often reserved for romantic context.
- **thương** — deep caring love with a protective quality. Used for family (parents, siblings, children), also for a partner you've grown deeply attached to. Combines love, compassion, and protectiveness. A mother "thương" her child. A grandfather "thương" his grandchild.
- **mến** — warm fondness, friendly affection. You "mến" a close friend, a kind neighbor. Not romantic.
- **quý** — to cherish/value someone. Often respect-based love. You "quý" a mentor, an elder. Implies the other person's worth.
- **thích** — to like. The lightest form of positive feeling. Can be used romantically early on ("I like you"), or for things/hobbies.
- **NUANCE**: A Vietnamese child says "con thương ba mẹ" (I thương my parents) — never "yêu", which would feel strange for family.

**Tamil (20+ love-related words)**
- **அன்பு (anbu)** — unconditional love, pure affection, the foundation of all love. Used for family, friends, divine.
- **காதல் (kadhal)** — romantic love, passionate, often with longing. The word for being "in love".
- **பாசம் (paasam)** — emotional bond/attachment, familial love with a clinging quality. Strong bond between siblings, parent-child.
- **நேசம் (nesam)** — friendship-based love, platonic warmth between close friends.
- **மோகம் (moham)** — infatuation, desire, sometimes with attachment or obsession. Used carefully.
- **ஆசை (aasai)** — desire, longing, wanting. Can mean wanting a person or wanting something.
- **வாஞ்சை (vaanjsai)** — deep longing for someone absent, a yearning love across distance.
- **கருணை (karunai)** — compassionate love, empathy-love. What you feel seeing someone suffer.
- **பிரியம் (priyam)** — fondness, preference. "I'm fond of you."
- **ஈர்ப்பு (eerpu)** — attraction, magnetic pull toward someone.
- **NUANCE**: Tamil literature (Sangam poetry) has an entire system called "akam" (inner poetry) with 5 landscapes (tinai), each representing a different phase of love — separation, union, infidelity, patient waiting, complaint.

**Arabic**
- **حب (hubb)** — love in general, the most common word
- **عشق (ishq)** — intense passionate love, often overwhelming, sometimes with spiritual dimension in Sufi poetry
- **مودة (mawadda)** — affection and warmth, the love that sustains a marriage (Quran uses this for spouses)
- **رحمة (rahma)** — compassionate love, mercy-love (also Quran for spouses). The word for mercy is the same root.
- **غرام (gharam)** — passionate attachment, longing love. "Maktub gharam" — written longing.
- **هوى (hawa)** — desire, inclination, can lead astray — more the pull of emotion
- **وُدّ (wudd)** — gentle, steady fond love between friends or family
- **NUANCE**: Arabic has a famous progression of love stages in classical poetry: hawa (inclination) → ishq (passion) → wajd (ecstasy) → hayam (obsession) → walah (grief-love) → shaghaf (heart-piercing love) → tatayum (enslavement of the heart).

**Greek (7 classical types of love)**
- **Eros (ἔρως)** — romantic, passionate love, physical attraction. Root of "erotic".
- **Philia (φιλία)** — friendship love, warm affection between equals. Deep loyalty.
- **Storge (στοργή)** — familial love, natural affection especially parent-child. Also the love between old friends.
- **Agape (ἀγάπη)** — unconditional love, selfless, the highest form. In Christianity: God's love for humans.
- **Pragma (πράγμα)** — mature, long-term love built over years. The love that endures.
- **Ludus (λούδος)** — playful, flirtatious love. Early dating, banter, teasing affection.
- **Philautia (φιλαυτία)** — self-love. Aristotle argued healthy self-love is the basis of loving others.
- **NUANCE**: Modern Greek uses "αγαπώ" (agapo) for love in many contexts, but the classical distinctions remain culturally understood.

**Japanese** (bonus)
- **愛 (ai)** — general love, often weighty and serious
- **恋 (koi)** — romantic love with longing, often for someone you haven't fully reached yet
- **好き (suki)** — like, fond of — the gentle entry point
- **NUANCE**: Japanese people often say "suki" rather than "ai shiteru" even in relationships, because "ai shiteru" feels intensely serious.

## CHUNK: sehnsucht | longing | nỗi nhớ | hiraeth | saudade

### Words for Longing That Don't Translate

**German**
- **Sehnsucht** — one of the most famous untranslatable words. A deep, bittersweet longing for something undefined — a place, a time, a person, a feeling. Not just missing someone. A yearning for what could be, what was, or what might never be.
- **Heimweh** — homesickness specifically. Longing for home.
- **Fernweh** — the opposite of Heimweh. Longing to be far away, wanderlust.
- **Weltschmerz** — pain from the gap between the world as it is and as it should be.

**Vietnamese**
- **Nỗi nhớ** — the feeling of missing someone/something. Can be used for people, places, food.
- **Nhớ nhà** — homesickness (missing home)
- **Hoài niệm** — nostalgia, missing the past with bittersweet warmth
- **NUANCE**: Vietnamese culture expresses longing often through music (nhạc vàng) and poetry — longing is aestheticized, not just felt.

**Portuguese**
- **Saudade** — perhaps the most famous longing word. A melancholic longing for something loved that is absent, may never return, or may never have existed. The very feeling of saudade is itself pleasurable.

**Welsh**
- **Hiraeth** — grief for lost places, people, times. Longing for a Wales (or home) that may no longer exist or may never have existed exactly as remembered.

**CULTURAL NOTE**: German Sehnsucht, Portuguese Saudade, Welsh Hiraeth, and Vietnamese Nỗi nhớ are cousins. Each culture has developed language for this human experience of longing, but each with different textures — German leans toward the transcendent, Vietnamese toward the relational, Portuguese toward the pleasurably melancholic.

## CHUNK: shame | scham | xấu hổ | arabic | japanese | cultural

### Shame Across Cultures

Shame (Scham/xấu hổ/عار) is one of the most culturally variable emotions.

**German**
- **Scham** — shame. Personal, internal. Something you feel when your actions violate your own values.
- **Peinlichkeit** — embarrassment. Social, external. When others see something awkward.
- CULTURAL NOTE: German culture distinguishes strongly between guilt (Schuld = I did something wrong) and shame (Scham = I am wrong). Therapy culture in Germany increasingly helps people recognize shame patterns.

**Vietnamese**
- **Xấu hổ** — shame/embarrassment (literally "ugly-afraid"). Deeply social — tied to losing face.
- **Mắc cỡ** — being shy or bashful, lighter version of xấu hổ
- **Mất mặt** — "to lose face" — the social catastrophe of public shame
- **Làm mặt** — to save face — the social choreography of shame management
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese shame is primarily collective and social — it reflects on family, not just the individual. "Con làm xấu mặt gia đình" = the child shamed the family's face. This creates different dynamics around shame than in individualist cultures.

**Arabic**
- **عار (aar)** — shame/disgrace, deeply social and familial
- **حياء (haya)** — modesty/shyness, considered a virtue in Islam
- CULTURAL NOTE: Haya (modesty) in Arab culture is often viewed positively — it's the restraint that maintains dignity.

**Japanese**
- **恥 (haji)** — shame, loss of social standing
- **恥ずかしい (hazukashii)** — embarrassment/shame (feeling)
- CULTURAL NOTE: Japanese culture has a famous "shame culture" vs Western "guilt culture" distinction (Ruth Benedict). Behavior is regulated by what others will think, not just internal conscience. This affects how apology and repair function.

### Anger and Its Cultural Expression

**German**
- **Wut** — anger, rage. Intense, often physical feeling.
- **Ärger** — annoyance, irritation. Milder form.
- **Zorn** — wrath. More dignified, righteous anger. (Biblical/literary)
- **Empörung** — outrage, moral indignation.
- **Frustration** — frustration (borrowed from English/Latin)
- CULTURAL NOTE: Germans are often perceived as direct in expressing displeasure, which comes across as anger to cultures where indirect communication is the norm (e.g., Vietnamese). What a German calls "honest feedback" a Vietnamese colleague may experience as aggressive.

**Vietnamese**
- **Tức giận** — angry (general)
- **Bực bội** — frustrated, irritated, pent-up
- **Phẫn nộ** — righteous indignation, outrage
- **Giận dỗi** — being sulky/upset in a relationship — silent treatment with hurt underneath
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture tends to suppress direct anger expression in public or with superiors. "Giận cá chém thớt" (angry at the fish, chop the cutting board) — displacing anger. Harmony (hòa thuận) is a core value; visible anger threatens it.

**Tamil**
- **கோபம் (kopam)** — anger, general
- **ஆத்திரம் (aathiram)** — intense rage, hot anger
- **சினம் (sinam)** — wrath, literary/classical term
- **பொறாமை (poramai)** — jealousy/envy-anger
- CULTURAL NOTE: Tamil Sangam poetry treats anger (sinnam) in a beloved as a sign of love — "the anger of love is sweeter than peace."

### The Feeling of Safe Belonging

**German**
- **Geborgenheit** — the feeling of being safely held, sheltered, warm. Home, family, a trusted person. One of the most important German emotional words and has no direct English equivalent. "Sich geborgen fühlen" — to feel held/sheltered/safe.
- Related to: Nestwärme (nest-warmth = family warmth), Heimeligkeit (cozy homeliness)

**Vietnamese**
- **Ấm áp** — warm (literally and emotionally). The warmth of being cared for.
- **Bình an** — peace, inner calm and safety
- **Được yêu thương** — being loved and cared for (compound of yêu + thương — romantic love + caring love together)
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese family warmth (sự ấm cúng gia đình) is intensely collective — you feel safe because you belong to the group, not because of individual sanctuary.

**Tamil**
- **சேர்ந்திருக்கும் உணர்வு** — the feeling of being together / belonging
- **அன்பான சூழல்** — loving environment/atmosphere
- CULTURAL NOTE: Tamil concept of "ஊர்" (ur — village/community) carries a sense of collective belonging that city life gradually erodes. A key source of Geborgenheit in Tamil culture is the extended family system.

### Gratitude Across Cultures

**German**
- **Dankbarkeit** — gratitude
- **Dankbarkeit empfinden** — to feel/experience gratitude
- CULTURAL NOTE: Germans tend to express gratitude more formally than southern Europeans or Americans. A heartfelt "Danke" with eye contact means more than repeated casual thanks.

**Vietnamese**
- **Lòng biết ơn** — gratitude (literally "heart knowing grace/favor")
- **Cảm ơn** — thank you (everyday)
- **Mang ơn** — to carry a debt of gratitude; to owe someone for their kindness
- **Ơn nghĩa** — the moral weight of a received kindness that creates obligation
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture has a deep concept of "ơn" (grace/favor received). When someone helps you, you "mang ơn" — carry their kindness. This creates long-term relational bonds. Gratitude is not just a feeling but a social contract.

**Tamil**
- **நன்றி (nandri)** — thank you / gratitude
- **கடன் (kadan)** — debt, obligation — receiving help creates a moral debt
- **உதவி (udhavi)** — help/assistance — the thing you're grateful for

## CHUNK: loneliness | einsamkeit | cô đơn | isolation | connection | belonging

### Loneliness — The Most Undernamed Emotion

Loneliness is one of the most widespread but least spoken emotions. It is not the same as being alone (solitude).

**German**
- **Einsamkeit** — loneliness. Being alone in a painful sense. Distinct from "Alleinsein" (being alone by choice).
- **Verlassenheit** — feeling abandoned, left behind. More acute than loneliness — implies someone left.
- **Verbundenheit** — connectedness, the opposite of loneliness. Feeling bonded to someone or a group.
- **Einsamkeit in der Menge** — "loneliness in the crowd" — feeling invisible while surrounded by people. A distinctly modern experience.
- CULTURAL NOTE: Germany has a long philosophical tradition around solitude (Rilke: "It is good to be solitary, for solitude is difficult"). German culture makes a strong distinction between chosen solitude (enriching) and imposed loneliness (painful).

**Vietnamese**
- **Cô đơn** — lonely/alone. Both the state of being alone and the feeling of loneliness.
- **Cô độc** — solitary, isolated — more severe or literary form
- **Trống vắng** — empty, hollow — the feeling left behind when someone is absent
- **Nhớ** — missing someone; loneliness is often expressed as missing a specific person rather than abstract aloneness
- CULTURAL NOTE: In Vietnamese collectivist culture, loneliness is rarely romanticized. To be alone is often seen as a problem to be solved by family or community. The concept of "thiếu thốn tình cảm" (lacking emotional warmth) describes the loneliness of not being loved or cared for.

**Greek**
- **Μοναξιά (monaxia)** — loneliness
- **Ερημιά (erimia)** — solitude/desolation (literally "desert" — the feeling of vast emptiness)
- CULTURAL NOTE: Greek culture strongly values parea (παρέα — good company, group of friends sharing time together). Loneliness is considered almost a social failure — you should always have your parea. This makes it harder to admit.

## CHUNK: guilt | schuld | shame | scham | difference | unterschied | vietnamese | arabic

### Guilt vs Shame — The Most Important Emotional Distinction

The difference between guilt and shame is one of the most important concepts in emotional literacy. They feel similar but work completely differently.

**The Core Distinction**
- **Guilt** (Schuld): "I did something bad." — Behavior-focused. The self is intact; the action was wrong. Guilt motivates repair: apologize, make amends, change behavior.
- **Shame** (Scham): "I am bad." — Identity-focused. The whole self feels defective, exposed, worthless. Shame motivates hiding, not repair.

**German**
- **Schuld** — guilt; also "debt" and "fault." German collapses all three into one word, which is philosophically rich.
- **Scham** — shame. The desire to disappear, to not be seen.
- **Reue** — remorse, regret — a form of guilt mixed with grief for what was done
- **Schuldgefühl** — feeling of guilt (compound: Schuld + Gefühl)
- CULTURAL NOTE: German psychological culture increasingly distinguishes healthy guilt (motivates growth) from toxic shame (damages self-worth). The Lutheran tradition historically blended both.

**Vietnamese**
- **Tội lỗi** — guilt, sin. Religious/moral weight.
- **Xấu hổ** — shame. Literally "ugly-afraid" — the social exposure of being seen as wrong.
- **Hối hận** — remorse, regret for past actions
- **Mặc cảm** — inferiority complex, chronic shame — feeling fundamentally less-than
- CULTURAL NOTE: In Vietnamese collectivist culture, both guilt and shame are amplified by their impact on the family group. "Làm mất mặt gia đình" (making the family lose face) is the ultimate shame — not just personal. This creates a specific form of family-shame that individual therapy often misses.

**Arabic**
- **ذنب (dhanb)** — guilt, sin
- **خزي (khizy)** — shame, disgrace
- **حياء (haya)** — modesty/shyness as virtue — a positive form of shame-awareness
- CULTURAL NOTE: In Islamic ethics, "haya" (modesty) is considered a pillar of good character — a sensitivity to how one appears before God and community. This gives shame a positive valence that Western psychology often lacks.

**PRACTICAL NOTE**: Therapists and educators worldwide now teach this distinction. When someone says "I feel so ashamed" — they usually need compassion and reconnection, not problem-solving. When someone says "I feel guilty" — they often need help with repair and action.

### Pride, Jealousy, and Envy — The Complex Emotions

These emotions are often confused or avoided, but are deeply important for honest emotional literacy.

**PRIDE (Stolz)**

**German**
- **Stolz** — pride. Can be healthy (achievement, identity) or problematic (arrogance).
- **Selbstwertgefühl** — self-worth, self-esteem — the stable version of pride as a baseline
- **Hochmut** — arrogance, hubris — pride that looks down on others
- **Bescheidenheit** — modesty, humility — the cultural counter to excessive pride

**Vietnamese**
- **Tự hào** — pride, especially collective/cultural pride ("tự hào là người Việt Nam" — proud to be Vietnamese)
- **Kiêu ngạo** — arrogance, pride that becomes problematic
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture expresses pride primarily through the collective — pride in family, nation, heritage. Individual pride (boasting about oneself) is viewed as arrogance. "Con ngoan của cha mẹ" (a good child to the parents) is a key Vietnamese pride identity.

**Greek**
- **Υπερηφάνεια (yperifania)** — pride (positive)
- **Αλαζονεία (alazoneia)** — arrogance, hubris
- **Φιλότιμο (philotimo)** — the Greek concept connecting pride, honor, and duty — acting with dignity

**JEALOUSY vs ENVY**

These are often confused but are distinct:
- **Jealousy** (Eifersucht): Fear of LOSING something you have (a partner, a relationship, a position). "I fear you'll take what's mine."
- **Envy** (Neid): Wanting what SOMEONE ELSE has. "I want what you have."

**German**
- **Eifersucht** — jealousy (romantic, competitive). Literally "eagerness-addiction."
- **Neid** — envy. Wanting what others have.
- **Missgunst** — begrudging envy — not just wanting what they have, but not wanting them to have it either

**Vietnamese**
- **Ghen** — jealousy (especially romantic). "Ghen tuông" — the full expression of romantic jealousy.
- **Đố kỵ** — envy
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture has a concept "ghen ăn tức ở" — jealousy about food and living (literal), meaning petty envy about material things. It's used semi-humorously but names something real.

### Hope, Exhaustion, and the Space Between

**HOPE (Hoffnung)**

**German**
- **Hoffnung** — hope. Active, expectant. "Die Hoffnung stirbt zuletzt" — hope dies last (German proverb).
- **Zuversicht** — confidence, optimism. Stronger than hope — a sense that things will be okay.
- **Sehnsucht** — longing with hope embedded. Not just missing something, but hoping it could be.
- **Erwartung** — expectation, anticipation

**Vietnamese**
- **Hi vọng** — hope
- **Hy vọng** — alternative spelling, same meaning. To cherish hope.
- **Mong muốn** — to wish/desire — hope with an active wanting
- **Tin tưởng** — trust/faith — hope grounded in trust in someone
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture grounds hope strongly in family and collective effort. "Không có gì là không thể" (nothing is impossible) is a common expression of active hope. Vietnamese refugee narratives are saturated with hope-through-hardship.

**EXHAUSTION / OVERWHELM**

**German**
- **Erschöpfung** — exhaustion, depletion. Physical and emotional emptiness.
- **Überwältigung** — overwhelm. Being "overpowered." Too much to process.
- **Burnout** — adopted from English; becoming more culturally recognized
- **Überforderung** — being overtaxed, asked to do more than you can handle
- CULTURAL NOTE: Germany has a complex relationship with exhaustion. The culture values hard work (Fleiß) but increasingly recognizes mental health needs. "Burnout" was adopted into German as a medical term decades before it gained acceptance in many other countries.

**Vietnamese**
- **Kiệt sức** — exhausted, drained (kiet = depleted, suc = strength/energy)
- **Mệt mỏi** — tired, weary — both physical and emotional tiredness
- **Áp lực** — pressure, stress — the feeling of being pressed down
- **Căng thẳng** — tension, stress
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture has a tradition of "chịu đựng" (endurance, bearing difficulty without complaint). This means exhaustion is often hidden or minimized — expressing it feels like weakness or burdening others.

**RESILIENCE VOCABULARY**
- German: **Resilienz**, **Widerstandskraft** (resistance-strength), **Stärke**
- Vietnamese: **Kiên cường** (resilient, steadfast), **Vượt qua** (to overcome), **Mạnh mẽ** (strong)

## CHUNK: vietnamese culture | collectivism | family | harmony | đạo hiếu | filial piety | relationships

### Vietnamese Emotional Culture — Deep Structure

Understanding Vietnamese emotional life requires understanding its collectivist foundation.

**Core Cultural Values**
- **Đạo hiếu (filial piety)** — the most fundamental Vietnamese value. Respect, care, and obligation toward parents and ancestors. Children are not just loved — they carry the family's future and honor. This shapes every emotional relationship.
- **Thể diện / Mặt mũi (face)** — social reputation, honor. Not just avoiding embarrassment but actively maintaining the family's dignity in community eyes. Much emotion is processed through "what will people say."
- **Hòa thuận (harmony)** — the value of peaceful, conflict-free relationships. Strong emotions (anger, conflict) are often suppressed to maintain harmony. Indirect communication is the norm.
- **Tình nghĩa (relational loyalty)** — the bond between people built through shared history, kindness, and obligation. Friendship and love in Vietnamese culture are not just feelings — they are commitments built over time.

**How Vietnamese Emotional Expression Differs from German**
- Germans tend toward directness (sagen was man denkt — say what you think). Vietnamese culture uses indirection, suggestion, and context.
- Vietnamese emotional vocabulary is more relational — emotions are often named in terms of WHO they're felt toward ("nhớ mẹ" — miss mom, not abstract "I miss someone").
- Public emotional expression: Germans more comfortable with direct emotional statements. Vietnamese express strong emotions primarily within trusted relationships.
- Aging and care: Vietnamese culture embeds care of elderly parents as a moral obligation (đạo hiếu), not a choice — this creates specific emotional dynamics around duty, obligation, and love.

**Key Untranslatable Concepts**
- **Tình (tình cảm)** — emotional warmth, feeling, affection. The glue of Vietnamese relationships.
- **Duyên** — fate, destiny — the invisible thread connecting people. "Có duyên mới gặp" (only those with fate/duyên meet). Used for romantic connections and deep friendships.
- **Ơn** — grace received, favor that creates moral debt. When someone helps you deeply, you "mang ơn" (carry their grace).

### Emotions and the Body — Somatic Intelligence

Emotions are not just in the mind. Research (and ancient wisdom) shows they live in the body.

**Key Concepts**

**German**
- **Körpergefühl** — body awareness, the felt sense of one's own physical state
- **Bauchgefühl** — gut feeling (Bauch = stomach). Intuition felt as a physical sensation.
- **Leibgefühl** — the felt sense of being in a body (philosophical/phenomenological term)
- **Herzklopfen** — heart pounding (Herz = heart, klopfen = knock/pound) — the physical sensation of excitement or fear
- **Gänsehaut** — goosebumps (Gänsehaut = goose skin) — the body's response to awe, music, cold, or fear

**Vietnamese**
- **Nao lòng** — a heart-stirring, gut-dropping feeling (lòng = heart/gut center)
- **Thắt lòng** — heart constriction — physical tightness in chest from grief or worry
- **Rùng mình** — shudder, physical full-body sensation from fear or awe
- **Bồn chồn** — restlessness, a physical agitation — can't sit still because of anxiety or anticipation

**The Body Map of Emotions (Research)**
Neuroscientist Lauri Nummenmaa mapped where emotions are felt in the body (2014):
- **Anger**: upper chest, arms (heat, pressure — activation for fighting)
- **Fear**: chest, stomach, legs (alert, ready to flee — "stomach drops")
- **Sadness**: chest, throat, eyes (heaviness, constriction)
- **Love**: whole body, especially chest (warmth and expansion)
- **Disgust**: throat, stomach (nausea, rejection)
- **Joy**: whole body, especially face (lightness, activation)

**Why This Matters for Emotional Literacy**
Many people first notice emotions as body sensations before having words. Teaching somatic awareness — "where do you feel that in your body?" — is often more accessible than abstract emotion naming. This is especially true across language barriers.

**CULTURAL NOTE**: Asian somatic traditions (Chinese medicine, Ayurveda, Vietnamese folk medicine) have long mapped emotions to organs — grief in the lungs, fear in the kidneys, anger in the liver. ### Fear and Anxiety Across Cultures

Fear is universal, but what cultures fear, name, and normalize varies enormously.

**German**
- **Angst** — fear/anxiety. Famous worldwide (Freud, Kierkegaard). Broader than English "fear" — includes existential dread, free-floating anxiety.
- **Furcht** — concrete fear of a specific thing (fear OF spiders)
- **Panik** — panic
- **Unbehagen** — unease, discomfort, a mild but persistent sense that something is off
- **Existenzangst** — existential anxiety; fear about the meaning and security of one's existence
- CULTURAL NOTE: "Angst" entered English and psychology because German philosophy (Kierkegaard wrote in Danish/German tradition, Freud in German) explored it as a fundamental human condition. In German, Angst is not shameful — it is philosophically respected.

**Vietnamese**
- **Sợ** — afraid (general)
- **Sợ hãi** — fear (intensified form)
- **Lo lắng** — worry, anxious concern (more common in everyday speech than sợ)
- **Hoảng sợ** — panic, terror
- **Bất an** — unease, insecurity (literally "not peaceful")
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese culture tends to express anxiety as "lo" (worry) rather than "sợ" (fear). "Lo cho con" (worry for the child) is a constant parental state — it signals love. Anxiety-as-love is normalized.

**Turkish**
- **Korku** — fear
- **Endişe** — worry, anxiety
- **Hüzün** — one of the most important Turkish emotional concepts: a communal melancholy, a form of wistfulness tinged with loss. Orhan Pamuk describes it as the defining emotion of Istanbul — not individual sadness but a collective, citywide mood of beautiful sadness.
- **Keder** — sorrow, deep grief
- CULTURAL NOTE: Hüzün is specifically not private sadness. It is shared, communal, almost proud. In Turkish culture, especially in Istanbul, it carries a sense of spiritual depth — those who feel hüzün deeply are considered more sensitive souls.

**Greek**
- **Φόβος (fovos)** — fear
- **Άγχος (agchos)** — anxiety (modern Greek borrowed this from ancient Greek "anguish")
- **Δέος (deos)** — awe/dread — the fear mixed with reverence (facing the sacred)
- **Τρόμος (tromos)** — terror, dread
- CULTURAL NOTE: Ancient Greek "phobos" was also the name of the god of fear (son of Ares). The Greeks distinguished between phobos (flight-inducing fear) and deimos (dread/panic). Modern Greek carries these ancient emotional weights.

**Tamil**
- **பயம் (payam)** — fear
- **அச்சம் (achcham)** — fear, dread (classical/literary)
- **கவலை (kavalai)** — worry, concern
- **திகில் (thigil)** — terror, horror
- CULTURAL NOTE: Tamil Sangam literature lists "achcham" (fear) as one of the 8 basic emotions (அஷ்ட பாவங்கள்). Classical Tamil had a sophisticated emotional taxonomy predating modern psychology.

### Greek Emotional Concepts — A World of Their Own

Greek has some of the richest emotional vocabulary in any language, both classical and modern.

**Core Modern Greek Emotional Concepts**
- **Φιλότιμο (philotimo)** — literally "love of honor." One of the most important Greek values: a sense of duty, pride, generosity, and honor all in one. Acting with philotimo means doing the right thing without being asked — helping a stranger, paying for others' meals, defending family — out of inner dignity, not obligation.
- **Μεράκι (meraki)** — doing something with soul, creativity, and love. When you put a piece of yourself into your work. A craftsman has meraki. A cook who makes food with love has meraki. Can't be faked.
- **Κέφι (kefi)** — a spirit of joy, high spirits, spontaneous celebration. The feeling at a good party, a shared feast, music that makes you want to dance. More than happiness — it is communal aliveness.
- **Λεβεντιά (leventia)** — bravery combined with grace and generosity. The quality of a noble, free-spirited person who acts with courage and beauty.
- **Στενοχώρια (stenochoria)** — literally "narrow/tight place." Distress, sadness, frustration — the feeling of being constrained. "Έχω στενοχώρια" = I'm distressed/sad.
- **Καημός (kaimos)** — deep longing, heartache, a burning desire for something lost or unreachable. More intense than nostalgia.
- **Σπλάχνα (splachna)** — compassion from the gut; deep empathy that moves you. (The same root as "splanchnic" in anatomy — the gut.) To feel someone's pain in your own body.

**Classical Greek Emotional Concepts**
- **Ἀλεξιθυμία (alexithymia)** — "no words for emotions" (a = without, lexis = word, thymos = soul/emotion). A modern psychological term coined from Greek roots in 1973. The inability to identify and describe one's own emotions. **This app is the exact opposite of alexithymia.**
- **Catharsis (κάθαρσις)** — the purification and release of emotion through art, drama, or story. Aristotle's concept: tragedy doesn't just make you feel — it purges and heals.
- **Eudaimonia (εὐδαιμονία)** — flourishing, a life well-lived. Not just happiness (hedone) but the deep satisfaction of living according to your values.

**CULTURAL NOTE**: Greek emotional culture integrates feeling into life visibly — grief is loud (keening at funerals), joy is communal (dancing, feasting). There is less separation between inner experience and outer expression than in northern European cultures.

### Trust and Connection

**German**
- **Vertrauen** — trust. Also: confidence, faith in a person or system.
- **Zutrauen** — to trust someone's capability; believing someone can do something
- **Verlässlichkeit** — reliability, the quality of being dependable
- **Verbundenheit** — connectedness, the feeling of being bonded/linked to someone
- **Zusammengehörigkeit** — belonging together; the sense of being part of a group

**Vietnamese**
- **Tin tưởng** — trust, confidence in someone
- **Tin cậy** — to rely on; trustworthiness (more about reliability)
- **Gắn bó** — deep attachment, bonding. The kind of connection built through shared time and difficulty.
- **Tình nghĩa** — the loyalty and moral bond between people who have been through things together
- CULTURAL NOTE: Vietnamese trust is built slowly through shared meals, family ties, and demonstrated loyalty over time. Trust between strangers is rare; trust within the group (gia đình, bạn thân) is deep. This shapes how Vietnamese people form friendships — fewer but deeper.

**Greek**
- **Εμπιστοσύνη (empistosyni)** — trust
- **Αξιοπιστία (axiopistia)** — trustworthiness, reliability
- **Φιλία (filia)** — friendship; Aristotle's deep conception of friendship as mutual virtue and goodwill
- CULTURAL NOTE: Aristotle distinguished three types of friendship (Nicomachean Ethics): friendships of utility, of pleasure, and of virtue. Only the third — where two people admire each other's character — is true friendship. This concept is embedded in Greek thinking about connection.

**Tamil**
- **நம்பிக்கை (nambikkai)** — trust, faith, hope (one word for all three)
- **அன்பு (anbu)** — unconditional love that forms the basis of trust
- **உறவு (uravu)** — relationship, kinship — trust embedded in relation
